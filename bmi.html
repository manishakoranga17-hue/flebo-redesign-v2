What is BMI?

BMI serves as a measure of an individual's body composition, taking into account their height and weight to quantify tissue mass. Its primary purpose is to assess whether a person maintains a healthy body weight relative to their height.

The calculated BMI value is instrumental in categorising individuals into different weight classifications — including underweight, normal weight, overweight, or obese — based on predetermined ranges. These classifications may further include distinctions such as severely underweight or very severely obese, and they can vary depending on factors like region and age. Recognising that both being underweight and overweight can have notable health implications, BMI acts as a valuable indicator, prompting the need for further evaluation or intervention.

For children and teens (2–19 years), BMI is interpreted using age- and sex-specific percentile charts instead of fixed ranges: below the 5th percentile is underweight, 5th–85th is a healthy weight, 85th–95th is overweight, and at or above the 95th percentile is obese.

Why a healthy BMI matters

Risks of being overweight

  • Type 2 diabetes
  • High blood pressure (hypertension)
  • Heart disease and stroke
  • Certain cancers
  • Osteoarthritis and joint pain
  • Obstructive sleep apnea
  • Gallbladder disease
  • Fatty liver disease

Risks of being underweight

  • Weakened immune system
  • Nutritional deficiencies and anaemia
  • Osteoporosis and weak bones
  • Fertility and menstrual issues
  • Persistent fatigue and low energy
  • Hair loss and dry skin
  • Delayed growth and development (in youth)
  • Higher risk of surgical complications

Limitations of BMI

  • BMI does not distinguish between fat and muscle, so very muscular people (e.g. athletes) can have a high BMI without excess fat.
  • It does not show where fat is stored — abdominal fat carries more health risk than fat elsewhere.
  • It is less reliable for pregnant women, the elderly, and people who have lost muscle mass.
  • For children and adolescents, age- and sex-specific percentile charts should be used instead of adult ranges.
  • Risk thresholds differ across ethnicities — South-Asian adults face higher risk at lower BMI values.

Other ways to assess body fat

Waist circumference

Measures abdominal fat. Above ~90 cm (men) or ~80 cm (women) for South-Asians signals raised health risk.

Waist-to-hip ratio

Waist divided by hip measurement. A higher ratio points to more central (abdominal) fat and greater risk.

Waist-to-height ratio

Keeping your waist under half your height is a simple, reliable indicator of a healthy body composition.

Keeping a healthy BMI

Move regularly

Aim for at least 150 minutes of moderate activity a week — brisk walks, cycling, or a sport you enjoy.

Eat balanced

Favour whole grains, vegetables, fruit, and protein; cut back on added sugar, fried food, and refined carbs.

Watch portions

Eat to about 80% full, stay hydrated, and avoid late-night overeating to keep your weight in a healthy range.
